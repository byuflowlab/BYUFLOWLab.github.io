HW 1: Configuration

due 9/20/2017 before midnight via Learning Suite 50 possible points

Reminder: Project homework is just like any other homework. You are welcome to work together, but each person must do their own work. Each person will need to know how to complete the assigned tasks.

Tip: Your design will evolve over the semester and so you will need to redo calculations. This will be easier if you perform any computations you might reuse in a programming language or in spreadsheets (with clear comments).

Project

  1. Choose your team and create a team name. Report your team name, the team leader, and the names of all members of the team.

  2. Choose the configuration for your aircraft (you aren’t locked in). In making your selection, evaluate at least three different aircraft configurations, and provide justification for your choice. You aren’t expected to know all the pros and cons of the various design choices, but do some research to help you understand some of the high-level tradeoffs. Some design choices to think about include, but are not limited to: tail vs canard vs flying wing, tail type (H, V, T, etc.), pusher vs tractor, fuselage type, materials, basic wing sizing (at least the wingspan), use of winglets or not, etc. Provide a top view (or even better a 3-view) of the design and some of the primary dimensions.

  3. Estimate the total mass of your aircraft in grams, including all electronic components. At this stage there are some things you can measure directly and others that you will need to estimate. Electrical components will all be (more or less) the same for all teams, but the airframe mass will differ depending on your specific design. Make estimates for each component (i.e., wing, horizontal tail, etc.), as opposed to the airframe all at once. Rough estimates are ok at this stage, but there needs to be a rational basis for the estimates.

General

  1. Consider a commercial transport with the following parameters:

    wing span 61 m
    reference wing area 325 m\(^2\)
    mass 200,000 kg
    Mach number 0.82
    zero lift drag coefficient 0.01
    Oswald efficiency factor 0.7

    We want to find the optimal altitude to fly at that will maximize specific range (which is the distance flown per unit weight of fuel burn). Assume a constant Mach number and steady, level flight. Later in the semester we will learn more about propulsion, but for now we will ignore changes in propulsive efficiency with altitude. Using that assumption implies that we should maximize the following metric:

    \[V \frac{L}{D}\]

    where \(V\) is the flight speed. Plot this metric as a function of altitude (interpretation of altitude is often more intuitive if plotted on the y-axis), and determine the optimal altitude.

    During the flight the aircraft will become lighter as it burns fuel. How would the optimal altitude change over time (stay the same, increase, decrease)? Why (provide a physical explanation)?
