ME EN 415: Flight Vehicle Design
Fall 2019
Andrew Ning

What is this course about?

The subject of this course is aircraft and rocket design. You will learn some of the basics of aerodynamics, structures, stability, propulsion, and performance as applied to flight vehicle design. In other words, you will be exposed to the fundamentals of aerospace engineering. A major component of the course is a hands-on, team project where you will design, build, and fly a small uninhabited aerial vehicle (UAV) to complete a specified mission. The UAV is not the goal of the class per se, but through the project you will be able to apply and demonstrate principles you learn in the class. While we don’t have time in one semester to also build a rocket, many of the concepts are transferable to rockets, and we will spend a few weeks specifically focused on rocket analysis and design.

How does this course relate to other courses?

The content of this class is the typical launching point for a mechanical engineer wishing to specialize in aerospace engineering. Subsequent courses provide greater depth on topics that we can only cover at an introductory level in this class: aerodynamics (515), gas turbine and jet engine design (426), compressible fluid flow (510), computational fluid dynamics (541), design optimization (575), composites (456, 602), aircraft structures (523), control systems (431), flight dynamics and control (634).

What are the prerequisites?

Formally, the listed prerequisites are: ME EN 273, ME EN 312. The latter may be taken concurrently, but you will need to read ahead to familiarize yourself with some concepts from that course. We do not assume any prior exposure to aircraft or spacecraft.

Some of you are from other departments, like physics. You’ll be just fine, though you should ask your classmates for help, as needed, in explaining some engineering concepts or terminology from fluids, dynamics, and structures that are re-used in this class.

What will the in-class experience be like?

Don’t expect a passive experience. I love this topic and can easily talk about it for 50 minutes, but it’s not going to be as great of a learning experience if you just listen to me talk the whole time. Instead, classes will be oriented around self-discovery. I’d actually prefer that you did not read the textbook beforehand. The concepts will stick with you better if you wrestle with them before learning the “right way” to think about them. To that end, in class I will ask lots of questions, ask you to predict things, draw charts, debate concepts with your neighbor, or solve problems that you haven’t seen before. Lecture will still be necessary, but will hopefully be reduced in favor of more time actively exploring. After class, the textbook is available to help you review or fill in details as needed. When possible I also plan to provide summary videos as a supplementary resource.

Please ask lots of questions! Especially ask the questions you think are “dumb” or “simple”, I guarantee someone else is wondering the same thing and you’re doing them a favor by asking. Some of the work we will do requires a computer. There are a few computers in the classroom that can be used, but if you have access to a laptop it will generally be helpful to bring it.

What textbook will we use?

I’ve developed a text specifically for this class that you can download here. I’ve developed a textbook because: 1) I’d like to eliminate textbook costs to save you money and redirect some of those costs towards purchasing UAV components (more on this below), 2) There is no one textbook at the appropriate level or with the right mix of topics for this class, and purchasing multiple textbooks would be expensive, and 3) tailoring the content to exactly what we need will make your study more efficient and allow for more effective exploration.

I do recommend Introduction to Flight by John Anderson (any edition) as a supplementary text. It is not necessary for the class, but will provide more context for those interested in exploring this topic further. The level of the text is a bit too basic for our needs, but does provide a nice introduction to a broad range of topics.

What about the labs?

The labs are supplementary—the lab hour is effectively just another office hour (it’s just a convenient time when many are available). The only purpose of the labs is to help streamline homework or the project. There is important information associated with the labs, but we will make that information available in the form of handouts/videos so nothing is missed by not being at the “lab hour”.

What are the material costs?

You will need to purchase most of the materials for your UAV (and will keep them). We will provide access to tools and a transmitter for use during the semester. You should not spend more than $50 per individual (or $250 per team).

What is the hardest aspect of this class?

One of the aspects students find hard about this class is that much of it is open ended. You may be used to, and comfortable with, prescribed approaches to solving homework problems. Aircraft design is complex, and most of the time I cannot provide you with a one-size-fits-all approach. Or, if I did, it would often be wrong and misleading. It’s a lot like the quote from Joseph Smith: ``I teach them correct principles, and they govern themselves.’’ You’ll learn principles in this class, and will need to wrestle some with how to apply them. Understandably, your inexperience will make it hard to always know how to use them, but we (instructor, TAs, fellow classmates) are here to help.

You should expect some messiness and set backs and will likely even crash your UAV into the ground! Don’t worry: this is normal, expected, and even desirable as part of the learning process. We tend to learn the most from our mistakes.

What is homework like and when is it due?

There will generally be a weekly assignment due every Wednesday at midnight (actually 11:59pm) via Learning Suite. Many of the problems will help you make progress on your project, though there will be some broader problems as well.

Last year’s assignments will still be up on the website for about a week so you can get a feel for them. This year’s assignments will be similar, but not exactly the same.

What about late work?

Late homework will be accepted with a penalty of 20% per day. Last minute issues often happen in life so be sure to start your work early and submit early! Despite your best planning you will likely have an unexpected event, obligation, or illness pop up. To help with this, I will give you three free late days. This policy is designed to help with unexpected issues so please don’t ask for homework extensions or other exceptions (unless you have an extreme or extended event like a hospitalization or death in the family).

Can I work with others? What about student work from prior years?

Working with others on homework is definitely encouraged, but you must do your own write-ups. Referring to solution manuals or student work from previous years is a violation of department policy and the honor code.

What format should the homework be in?

Assignments are expected to be produced in a report or memo format. A series of equations and “solutions” is insufficient. Each report should introduce the problem and any assumptions, detail the methodology, and discuss the results and conclusions (the IMRaD format). Here is an example solution from a question I asked in a different class. No need to go overboard with this, but in general, many students do not provide sufficient detail on their first homework and/or provide figures that are not clearly presented. Presenting results clearly is an important skill you need to learn as you transition to industry or graduate school. Clearly describing your work in the homeworks will also save you time when putting together the final project report.

How will we progress on the project?

It is up to you to exercise discipline and set aside regular time to make progress (even when the final deadline is far away). The homework assignments will often directly help, and there will be two design reviews with some associated milestones to help keep you on track.

What about exams?

There will be two midterm exams and one comprehensive final exam. The format has not yet been determined.

There will be periodic quizzes to help you and I gauge understanding and reinforce concepts. Quizzes will be low weight and will be graded solely on participation. Schedule a reminder because we won’t open up late quizzes after they are due (it becomes a logistical pain that isn’t justifiable giving the small number of points). Instead, I will drop two quizzes because we all forget or have emergencies occasionally.

How will I be graded?

Many (but not all) problems don’t have a “correct answer”, as is generally true in real-world engineering. Primarily we are looking for good engineering work: clarity, justifiable assumptions, technically sound procedures, critical evaluation of the results. Be sure that you address each item asked for in the problem statement.

Project milestones have rubrics attached to them.

The approximate percent breakdown is: 40% homework, 40% exams, 20% project.

Academic Honesty

The first injunction of the Honor Code is the call to “be honest.” Students come to the university not only to improve their minds, gain knowledge, and develop skills that will assist them in their life’s work, but also to build character. “President David O. McKay taught that character is the highest aim of education” (The Aims of a BYU Education, p.6). It is the purpose of the BYU Academic Honesty Policy to assist in fulfilling that aim. BYU students should seek to be totally honest in their dealings with others. They should complete their own work and be evaluated based upon that work. They should avoid academic dishonesty and misconduct in all its forms, including but not limited to plagiarism, fabrication or falsification, cheating, and other academic misconduct.

Plagiarism

Intentional plagiarism is a form of intellectual theft that violates widely recognized principles of academic integrity as well as the Honor Code. Such plagiarism may subject the student to appropriate disciplinary action administered through the university Honor Code Office, in addition to academic sanctions that may be applied by an instructor. Inadvertent plagiarism, which may not be a violation of the Honor Code, is nevertheless a form of intellectual carelessness that is unacceptable in the academic community. Plagiarism of any kind is completely contrary to the established practices of higher education where all members of the university are expected to acknowledge the original intellectual work of others that is included in their own work. In some cases, plagiarism may also involve violations of copyright law. Intentional Plagiarism-Intentional plagiarism is the deliberate act of representing the words, ideas, or data of another as one’s own without providing proper attribution to the author through quotation, reference, or footnote. Inadvertent Plagiarism-Inadvertent plagiarism involves the inappropriate, but non-deliberate, use of another’s words, ideas, or data without proper attribution. Inadvertent plagiarism usually results from an ignorant failure to follow established rules for documenting sources or from simply not being sufficiently careful in research and writing. Although not a violation of the Honor Code, inadvertent plagiarism is a form of academic misconduct for which an instructor can impose appropriate academic sanctions. Students who are in doubt as to whether they are providing proper attribution have the responsibility to consult with their instructor and obtain guidance. Examples of plagiarism include: Direct Plagiarism-The verbatim copying of an original source without acknowledging the source. Paraphrased Plagiarism-The paraphrasing, without acknowledgement, of ideas from another that the reader might mistake for the author’s own. Plagiarism Mosaic-The borrowing of words, ideas, or data from an original source and blending this original material with one’s own without acknowledging the source. Insufficient Acknowledgement-The partial or incomplete attribution of words, ideas, or data from an original source. Plagiarism may occur with respect to unpublished as well as published material. Copying another student’s work and submitting it as one’s own individual work without proper attribution is a serious form of plagiarism.
