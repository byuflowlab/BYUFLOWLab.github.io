Syllabus | Andrew Ning | Winter 2023

What is this course about?

This course is about aircraft and rocket design. You will learn some of the basics of aerodynamics, structures, stability, propulsion, and performance as applied to flight vehicle design. In other words, you will be exposed to the fundamentals of aerospace engineering. Rather than a deep dive into any one discipline, our focus is understanding tradeoffs amongst multiple disciplines to allow one to approach vehicle-level design problems. The class involves a combination of theory, analytic methods, computational tools, and a hands-on design project.

How does this course relate to other courses?

The content of this class is the typical launching point for a mechanical engineer wishing to specialize in aerospace engineering. Subsequent courses provide greater depth on topics that we can only cover at an introductory level in this class: gas turbine and jet engine design (426), compressible fluid flow (510), aerodynamics (515), computational fluid dynamics (541), design optimization (575), composites (456, 602), aircraft structures (523), control systems (431), flight dynamics and control (536), etc..

What are the prerequisites?

Formally, the listed prerequisites are: ME EN 273, ME EN 312. The latter may be taken concurrently, but you will need to read ahead to familiarize yourself with some concepts from that course. We do not assume any prior exposure to aircraft or spacecraft.

Some of you are from other departments, like physics. You’ll be just fine, though you should ask your classmates for help, as needed, in explaining some engineering concepts or terminology from fluids, dynamics, and structures that are re-used in this class.

What will the in-class experience be like?

Most classes will be oriented around self-discovery. I’d recommend that read the textbook or watch videos after lectures. The concepts will stick with you better if you wrestle with them before learning the “right way” to think about them. To that end, in class I will ask lots of questions, ask you to predict things, draw charts, debate concepts with your neighbor, or solve problems that you haven’t seen before. You should also ask lots of questions! The textbook and some pre-recorded videos are available to help you review or fill in details from lectures and class discussions.

What textbook will we use?

I’ve developed a text specifically for this class because: 1) I’d like to eliminate textbook costs to save you money and redirect some of those costs towards purchasing aircraft materials (less than $50), 2) There is no one textbook at the appropriate level or with the right mix of topics for this class, and purchasing multiple textbooks would be expensive, and 3) Tailoring the content to exactly what we need will make your study more efficient.

Introduction to Flight by John Anderson (any edition) is a good supplementary text. It is not necessary for the class, but will provide more context for those interested in exploring this topic further. The level of the text is a bit too basic for our needs, but does provide a nice introduction to a broad range of topics.

What assignments will we have?

There will generally be a weekly assignment due every Wednesday at midnight (actually 11:59pm) via Learning Suite. Make sure to show your work. This is particularly important for design problems, which do not have a “right” answer, and so need a little more detail on your assumptions, methods, justification, etc. Some of the assignments will be project oriented.

Late homework will be accepted with a penalty of 20% per day. Last minute issues often happen in life so be sure to start your work early and submit early! Despite your best planning you will likely have an unexpected event, obligation, or illness pop up. To help with this, I will give you three free late days (total per semester, not per assignment). This policy is designed to help with unexpected issues, so we generally won’t be granting other extensions (although if something major happens do come talk to us).

Working with others on homework is definitely encouraged, but you must do your own write-ups. Referring to solution manuals or student work from previous years is a violation of department policy and the honor code.

There will be two midterm exams and one comprehensive final exam, all timed and delivered via Learning Suite. There will be periodic quizzes to help you and I gauge understanding and reinforce concepts. Quizzes will be low weight. We won’t re-open late quizzes, but instead will drop two quizzes. Exams and quizzes must be done individually.

The approximate percent breakdown is: 40% homework, 40% exams, 20% project.

What are the course learning outcomes?

  • Aerodynamics: analyze and measure aerodynamic forces and moments.
  • Structures: analyze structural response to aerodynamic and inertial loads.
  • Stability: analyze longitudinal and lateral stability both statically and dynamically.
  • Propulsion: analyze thrust and propulsive efficiency.
  • Performance: select appropriate performance metrics, and evaluate performance.
  • Integrated Design: analyze and design a flight vehicle for a specified mission using a well-reasoned and clearly articulated approach.

Plagiarism

Intentional plagiarism is a form of intellectual theft that violates widely recognized principles of academic integrity as well as the Honor Code. Such plagiarism may subject the student to appropriate disciplinary action administered through the university Honor Code Office, in addition to academic sanctions that may be applied by an instructor. Inadvertent plagiarism, which may not be a violation of the Honor Code, is nevertheless a form of intellectual carelessness that is unacceptable in the academic community. Plagiarism of any kind is completely contrary to the established practices of higher education where all members of the university are expected to acknowledge the original intellectual work of others that is included in their own work. In some cases, plagiarism may also involve violations of copyright law. Intentional Plagiarism-Intentional plagiarism is the deliberate act of representing the words, ideas, or data of another as one’s own without providing proper attribution to the author through quotation, reference, or footnote. Inadvertent Plagiarism-Inadvertent plagiarism involves the inappropriate, but non-deliberate, use of another’s words, ideas, or data without proper attribution. Inadvertent plagiarism usually results from an ignorant failure to follow established rules for documenting sources or from simply not being sufficiently careful in research and writing. Although not a violation of the Honor Code, inadvertent plagiarism is a form of academic misconduct for which an instructor can impose appropriate academic sanctions. Students who are in doubt as to whether they are providing proper attribution have the responsibility to consult with their instructor and obtain guidance. Examples of plagiarism include: Direct Plagiarism-The verbatim copying of an original source without acknowledging the source. Paraphrased Plagiarism-The paraphrasing, without acknowledgement, of ideas from another that the reader might mistake for the author’s own. Plagiarism Mosaic-The borrowing of words, ideas, or data from an original source and blending this original material with one’s own without acknowledging the source. Insufficient Acknowledgement-The partial or incomplete attribution of words, ideas, or data from an original source. Plagiarism may occur with respect to unpublished as well as published material. Copying another student’s work and submitting it as one’s own individual work without proper attribution is a serious form of plagiarism.
