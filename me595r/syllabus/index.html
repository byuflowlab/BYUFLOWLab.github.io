What is this course about?

Amongst the various machine learning algorithms, neural nets have emerged as uniquely powerful and versatile. Their application in an engineering context often differs from traditional usage as there are critical physics constraints that need to be satisfied and well-developed mathematical algorithms we can leverage. Large volumes of data can be used to learn such constraints/procedures, but often such copious data isn’t available in engineering contexts. Even when such data is available, there usually exists important structure in engineering relationships (e.g., differential equations) that we can use to dramatically improve accuracy and/or speed of training. This class is about the emerging field at the intersection of neural nets and engineering.

What are the learning outcomes?

  • Mathematical Foundations: Explain and apply the mathematical foundations of deep learning, including backpropagation, optimization, and cross-validation.
  • Architectures: Design and implement engineering-focused architectures such as physics-informed neural networks (PINNs), neural ODEs, neural Koopman models, convolutional networks, graph networks, Kolmogorov-Arnold networks, and neural operators.
  • Incorporate Physics: Develop and train models that integrate prior knowledge, domain constraints, physical laws, differential equations, and/or other engineering and mathematical relationships.
  • Evaluate Model Suitability: Evaluate and verify model performance measures such as accuracy, uncertainty, generalization, interpretability, and scalability.
  • Assess Emerging Methods: Critically assess contemporary research in deep learning for scientific and engineering domains, including emerging architectures and methodologies.
  • Engineering Project: Formulate and execute a research or engineering project using advanced deep learning methods, demonstrating creativity, rigor, and domain relevance.
  • Software Tools: Utilize modern software tools and frameworks (e.g., PyTorch) and hardware accelerators (e.g., GPUs) to build scalable and reproducible deep learning workflows.

What are the prerequisites?

You should be comfortable with numerical methods, computer programming, and math (multivariable calculus, linear algebra, ordinary differential equations). We will be using Python as that is the language used for undergraduate numerical methods courses in Mechanical, Electrical, and Chemical Engineering as well as the intro CS programming courses. If you are familiar with a different language, that’s fine, but you should run through Python tutorials on your own to get comfortable with the syntax/workflow. We do not assume prior exposure to machine/deep learning.

Will I be fine to take this course as an undergraduate?

The workload of graduate courses is higher than that of undergraduate courses (approximately 4 hrs work per credit hour instead of 3). And the expected proficiency in computing and math is higher than that of undergraduate courses. That said, undergraduates who have the time, and meet the prerequisites, tend to do just as well as the graduate students. As an alternative, some undergraduates might be interested in taking one of the Senior-level engineering machine learning classes: EC EN 471 Machine Learning: Foundations and Applications or CH EN 426 Machine Learning for Engineers, or the Senior-level deep learning class in computer science: CS 474 Introduction to Deep Learning. The topics are not the same, but many of the foundations are similar, and the time commitment and proficiency level might be a more appropriate fit for some.

How is this course related to the machine learning classes in the college? the deep learning class in CS? the ME optimization class?

Machine learning is a broader field of many algorithms, neural nets are just one such option. Neural nets tend to be the most sophisticated, versatile, and powerful of the bunch, so much so that they’ve become their own subfield, often referred to as deep learning. However, you don’t always need the bigger machinery of neural nets for every problem, sometimes a simpler method is sufficient. A machine learning classes is not a prerequisite though these classes would help ease you into the general workflow of machine learning.

Deep learning as taught in a CS department will have similar fundamentals, and the basic algorithms like a vanilla neural network, and convolutional neural networks are the same. The rest is pretty different, and not just in applications. We focus on algorithms that are not just purely data-driven, but blend in known structure from the scientific problem at hand. Unlike text, audio, visual problems, most engineering problems have governing equations, physical constraints, or numerical solvers that we can leverage, in combination with neural net techniques, to develop engineering-specific approaches. That said, taking a CS deep learning class would help round out your exposure to deep-learning algorithms.

Optimization is not a prerequisite, though that background certainly is helpful. In optimization, you learn a broad set of optimization algorithms appropriate for a wide range of problems. Neural nets are often mentioned in an optimization course, at a basic level, as a type of surrogate that might be used in a broader optimization problem. Conversely, in deep learning we use a small subset of relatively simplistic optimization algorithms, but we explore many neural net architectures and approaches suitable for a wide range of deep learning applications.

What will the in-class experience be like?

It is well established that active learning helps us learn better than passive approaches. To that end, in class I will pose questions, ask you to predict things, discuss concepts with your neighbor, or attempt problems that you haven’t seen before. It can be a little uncomfortable to wrestle with things before understanding, but this helps concepts stick much better. I would encourage you to ask lots of questions!

What textbook will we use?

There isn’t an existing textbook we can use since this field is so new. The in-class presentations will be our primary source and annotated slides will be posted, though I’d highly recommend you also take your own notes. I hope to create supplementary lecture videos for most lecture topics this year (as time permits, no guarantees). Some supplementary readings will come from scholarly papers and various websites. There are also textbook chapters that cover some topics and these are linked in the reading (these longer chapters are considered optional reading).

What are the assignments?

A homework assignment will typically be due every week on Thursdays before midnight (11:59pm) via Learning Suite.

Towards the end of the class you will work on a project of your choosing (about the scope of three homework assignments). The final project deliverable will be a recorded video presentation.

Other assessments include low-weight weekly quizzes (quizzes can be taken twice), a midterm, and a comprehensive final exam.

What about late work?

Late homework will be accepted with a penalty of 20% per day. Last minute issues often happen in life so be sure to start your work early and submit early! Despite your best planning you will likely have an unexpected event, obligation, or illness pop up. To help with this, I will give you three free late days (total per semester, not per assignment). This policy is designed to help with unexpected issues, so we generally won’t grant other extensions unless of course there are prolonged/significant disruptions.

We won’t re-open late quizzes but instead will drop two quizzes because we all forget or have occasional emergencies.

How do I get help? Can I work with others? AI?

In addition to TA support, we have a Slack group for the class. Slack is a forum where you can post questions and get comments, suggestions, and tips from myself, the TAs, and fellow classmates. Note that joining the Slack group isn’t required (I will post any official announcements via Learning Suite), but it is recommended.

AI tools are particularly helpful with Python and PyTorch and I encourage learning to use them effectively. Never just copy and paste code, but instead go through each line and perhaps even type it yourself so that 1) you understand and learn from it, and 2) you make sure it does what you actually want to do (often you’ll need to make some small changes). You’ll likely have more success if you break up the problem into smaller pieces and ask for help on these smaller tasks (a good debugging strategy in general). When code comes up that you don’t understand you should consult the PyTorch documentation, ask the AI chatbots (they are also usually pretty good at explaining PyTorch code), or ask a question on Slack.

Finally, keep in mind that while working with others on homework is definitely encouraged, you must do your own analysis and your own write-ups. Whether working with classmates, TAs, or AI, it is not appropriate to merely seek out solutions but rather to use these resources to learn and build up understanding.

Plagiarism

Intentional plagiarism is a form of intellectual theft that violates widely recognized principles of academic integrity as well as the Honor Code. Such plagiarism may subject the student to appropriate disciplinary action administered through the university Honor Code Office, in addition to academic sanctions that may be applied by an instructor. Inadvertent plagiarism, which may not be a violation of the Honor Code, is nevertheless a form of intellectual carelessness that is unacceptable in the academic community. Plagiarism of any kind is completely contrary to the established practices of higher education where all members of the university are expected to acknowledge the original intellectual work of others that is included in their own work. In some cases, plagiarism may also involve violations of copyright law. Intentional Plagiarism-Intentional plagiarism is the deliberate act of representing the words, ideas, or data of another as one’s own without providing proper attribution to the author through quotation, reference, or footnote. Inadvertent Plagiarism-Inadvertent plagiarism involves the inappropriate, but non-deliberate, use of another’s words, ideas, or data without proper attribution. Inadvertent plagiarism usually results from an ignorant failure to follow established rules for documenting sources or from simply not being sufficiently careful in research and writing. Although not a violation of the Honor Code, inadvertent plagiarism is a form of academic misconduct for which an instructor can impose appropriate academic sanctions. Students who are in doubt as to whether they are providing proper attribution have the responsibility to consult with their instructor and obtain guidance. Examples of plagiarism include: Direct Plagiarism-The verbatim copying of an original source without acknowledging the source. Paraphrased Plagiarism-The paraphrasing, without acknowledgement, of ideas from another that the reader might mistake for the author’s own. Plagiarism Mosaic-The borrowing of words, ideas, or data from an original source and blending this original material with one’s own without acknowledging the source. Insufficient Acknowledgement-The partial or incomplete attribution of words, ideas, or data from an original source. Plagiarism may occur with respect to unpublished as well as published material. Copying another student’s work and submitting it as one’s own individual work without proper attribution is a serious form of plagiarism.
