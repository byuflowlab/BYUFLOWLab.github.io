Andrew Ning | Winter 2025

What is this course about?

Amongst the various machine learning algorithms, neural nets have emerged as uniquely powerful and versatile. Their application in an engineering context often differs from traditional usage as there are critical physics constraints that need to be satisfied. Large volumes of data can be used to learn such constraints, but often such copious data isn’t available in engineering contexts. Even when such data is available, there usually exists important structure in engineering relationships (e.g., differential equations) that we can use to dramatically improve accuracy and/or speed of training. So in a sentence this class is about the emerging field at the intersection of neural nets and engineering.

What are the prerequisites?

There are no listed prerequisites, but you should be comfortable with numerical methods, programming, and math. We will be using Python as that is the language now used for undergraduate numerical methods courses in Mechanical, Electrical, and Chemical Engineering as well as the intro CS programming courses. If you are familiar with a different language, that’s fine, but you should run through Python tutorials on your own to get comfortable with the syntax/workflow. We do not assume prior exposure to machine/deep learning.

What will the in-class experience be like?

It is well established that active learning helps us learn better than passive approaches. To that end, in class I will ask lots of questions, ask you to predict things, draw charts, debate concepts with your neighbor, or solve problems that you haven’t seen before. It can be a little uncomfortable to wrestle with things before understanding, but this helps concepts stick much better. You should also ask lots of questions!

This is a new class, so please be patient and expect some rough edges!

What textbook will we use?

There isn’t an existing textbook we can use since this field is so new. The in-class presentations will be our primary source. I’ll post annotated slides, but I’d recommend you also take your own notes. Some supplementary readings will come from scholarly papers and various websites. There are textbooks that cover some of the topics and these are linked under resources for those interested.

What are the assignments?

A homework assignment will typically be due every week on Thursdays before midnight (11:59pm) via Learning Suite. After submission, I will provide an example solution and you will grade yourself (using a coarse scale based on completion and effort).

Towards the end of the class you will work on a project of your choosing (about the scope of three homework assignments). The final project deliverable will be a recorded video presentation.

Other assessments include low-weight weekly quizzes (quizzes can be taken twice), a midterm, and a comprehensive final exam.

What about late work?

Late homework will be accepted with a penalty of 20% per day. Last minute issues often happen in life so be sure to start your work early and submit early! Despite your best planning you will likely have an unexpected event, obligation, or illness pop up. To help with this, I will give you three free late days (total per semester, not per assignment). This policy is designed to help with unexpected issues, so we generally won’t grant other extensions unless of course there are prolonged/significant disruptions.

We won’t re-open late quizzes as it is hard to do fairly, but instead will drop two quizzes because we all forget or have occasional emergencies.

How do I get help? Can I work with others? AI?

Getting TAs for a new class is challenging. I have a couple of TAs but they have limited hours and haven’t seen many of these topics yet either. However, they do have good experience with Python and PyTorch, and will be working through the homeworks beforehand, so they should be helpful to you.

In addition, I will setup a Slack group for the class. It is a helpful way to post questions and get comments, suggestions, and tips from myself, the TAs, and fellow classmates. Note that joining the Slack group isn’t required (I will post any official announcements via Learning Suite), but is highly recommended.

I would also recommend making good use of AI chatbots like ChatGPT or Copilot. These AI tools are particularly helpful with PyTorch. But of course like all AI chatbots you need to take care with the responses. Never blindly copy and paste code, but instead go through each line so that 1) you understand and learn from it, and 2) you make sure it does what you actually want to do (often you’ll need to make some small changes). You’ll likely have more success if you break up the problem into smaller pieces and ask for help on these smaller tasks (a good debugging strategy in general). When code comes up that you don’t understand you should consult the PyTorch documentation, ask the AI chatbots (they are also usually pretty good at explaining PyTorch code), or ask a question on Slack.

Finally, keep in mind that while working with others on homework is definitely encouraged, you must do your own analysis and your own write-ups. Whether working with classmates, TAs, or AI, it is not appropriate to merely seek out solutions but rather to use these resources to learn and build up understanding.

Plagiarism

Intentional plagiarism is a form of intellectual theft that violates widely recognized principles of academic integrity as well as the Honor Code. Such plagiarism may subject the student to appropriate disciplinary action administered through the university Honor Code Office, in addition to academic sanctions that may be applied by an instructor. Inadvertent plagiarism, which may not be a violation of the Honor Code, is nevertheless a form of intellectual carelessness that is unacceptable in the academic community. Plagiarism of any kind is completely contrary to the established practices of higher education where all members of the university are expected to acknowledge the original intellectual work of others that is included in their own work. In some cases, plagiarism may also involve violations of copyright law. Intentional Plagiarism-Intentional plagiarism is the deliberate act of representing the words, ideas, or data of another as one’s own without providing proper attribution to the author through quotation, reference, or footnote. Inadvertent Plagiarism-Inadvertent plagiarism involves the inappropriate, but non-deliberate, use of another’s words, ideas, or data without proper attribution. Inadvertent plagiarism usually results from an ignorant failure to follow established rules for documenting sources or from simply not being sufficiently careful in research and writing. Although not a violation of the Honor Code, inadvertent plagiarism is a form of academic misconduct for which an instructor can impose appropriate academic sanctions. Students who are in doubt as to whether they are providing proper attribution have the responsibility to consult with their instructor and obtain guidance. Examples of plagiarism include: Direct Plagiarism-The verbatim copying of an original source without acknowledging the source. Paraphrased Plagiarism-The paraphrasing, without acknowledgement, of ideas from another that the reader might mistake for the author’s own. Plagiarism Mosaic-The borrowing of words, ideas, or data from an original source and blending this original material with one’s own without acknowledging the source. Insufficient Acknowledgement-The partial or incomplete attribution of words, ideas, or data from an original source. Plagiarism may occur with respect to unpublished as well as published material. Copying another student’s work and submitting it as one’s own individual work without proper attribution is a serious form of plagiarism.
