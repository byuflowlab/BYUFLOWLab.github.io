What is this course about?

Computational methods are foundational in engineering, science, business, and are used across an ever expanding range of fields that impact our daily life. This course will provide an introduction to a broad set of well-used methods in engineering. Understanding these methods will massively enhance your engineering toolbox, and will lay a foundation for future studies in topics such as optimization, machine learning, computational fluid dynamics, robotics, etc.

Robust numerical libraries exist in most programming languages, and we will become familiar with some of these libraries in Python. However, to know when and how to use the methods effectively it helps to also understand how the algorithms work so we will also spend some time in the math and implementation.

What will the in-class experience be like?

Most classes will start with interactive discussions followed by time to practice implementations (individually or in small groups). Since we will spend a lot of time trying things out during class, you will want to bring a laptop. If not possible, you should find a friend that you can work with. Research suggests that we learn better when we wrestle with concepts before being shown the “right way”. Thus, I’d recommend reading or watching videos on the topics after lectures rather than before.

What about labs?

We won’t have a formal lab. Instead, the lab time will just be optional office hours. The content (or similar content) used for labs in other sections is just integrated into your weekly assignment.

What textbook will we use?

There isn’t a single textbook that covers the range of topics needed in this class. Rather than buy multiple books I’ll refer you to some freely available resources, and I will develop some additional notes when possible.

What are the assignments?

There will be a short quiz due every Tuesday at midnight (11:59pm) via Learning Suite. Quizzes are low-weight to help you and I gauge understanding and reinforce concepts. You are allowed to take a quiz twice. The first time should be closed-book. This helps you with retrieval, a practice that promotes learning. The second time is open-book and your score will be the best of the two attempts, which should be the latter!

There will be a weekly assignment due every Thursday at midnight. These will be submitted as a “quiz” on Learning Suite, except that unlike normal quizzes you can open and exit as much as you like before the deadline. Remember to make your figures clear. At a minimum that means that axis need to be labeled, and if multiple plots exist on a single graph they should be annotated or a legend should be used.

There will be an exploratory project with a written report, two midterm exams, and one comprehensive final exam. These assignments are weighted approximately as follows: homework (35%), project (5%), quizzes (10%), midterms (15% each), final (20%).

What about late work?

Late homework will be accepted with a penalty of 10% per day. Last minute issues often happen in life so be sure to start your work early and submit early! Despite your best planning you will likely have an unexpected event, obligation, or illness pop up. To help with this, I will give you three free late days (total per semester, not per assignment). This policy is designed to help with unexpected issues, so we generally won’t grant other extensions (unless of course there are prolonged/significant disruptions).

We won’t re-open late quizzes, but instead will drop two quizzes because we all forget or have occasional emergencies.

Can I work with others? AI? Prior work?

Working with others on homework is definitely encouraged, but you must do your own work and shouldn’t be sharing code or looking at others code. Generative AI, programming forums, and similar resources can be a helpful learning aid if used appropriately. However, it is a violation of department policy and the honor code to simply seek out solutions (and of course this robs you of the learning, which is the whole purpose of having a class). If you use AI you should interact with it like you would a TA. For example, don’t give it code and say how do I fix this. Instead, you could give it code and say things like: ask me guiding questions to help me figure this out. Or, as another example: after I explain my solution and thinking, can you give me feedback on my approach. AI will not be allowed on exams.

Plagiarism

Intentional plagiarism is a form of intellectual theft that violates widely recognized principles of academic integrity as well as the Honor Code. Such plagiarism may subject the student to appropriate disciplinary action administered through the university Honor Code Office, in addition to academic sanctions that may be applied by an instructor. Inadvertent plagiarism, which may not be a violation of the Honor Code, is nevertheless a form of intellectual carelessness that is unacceptable in the academic community. Plagiarism of any kind is completely contrary to the established practices of higher education where all members of the university are expected to acknowledge the original intellectual work of others that is included in their own work. In some cases, plagiarism may also involve violations of copyright law. Intentional Plagiarism-Intentional plagiarism is the deliberate act of representing the words, ideas, or data of another as one’s own without providing proper attribution to the author through quotation, reference, or footnote. Inadvertent Plagiarism-Inadvertent plagiarism involves the inappropriate, but non-deliberate, use of another’s words, ideas, or data without proper attribution. Inadvertent plagiarism usually results from an ignorant failure to follow established rules for documenting sources or from simply not being sufficiently careful in research and writing. Although not a violation of the Honor Code, inadvertent plagiarism is a form of academic misconduct for which an instructor can impose appropriate academic sanctions. Students who are in doubt as to whether they are providing proper attribution have the responsibility to consult with their instructor and obtain guidance. Examples of plagiarism include: Direct Plagiarism-The verbatim copying of an original source without acknowledging the source. Paraphrased Plagiarism-The paraphrasing, without acknowledgement, of ideas from another that the reader might mistake for the author’s own. Plagiarism Mosaic-The borrowing of words, ideas, or data from an original source and blending this original material with one’s own without acknowledging the source. Insufficient Acknowledgement-The partial or incomplete attribution of words, ideas, or data from an original source. Plagiarism may occur with respect to unpublished as well as published material. Copying another student’s work and submitting it as one’s own individual work without proper attribution is a serious form of plagiarism.
